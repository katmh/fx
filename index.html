Throughout the world, 1 in 3600 males and 1 in 4000 females suffer from fragile X syndrome (FXS), an inherited developmental disorder.

Symptoms range from intellectual disability and aggressive, hyperactive behavior to seizues and physical abnormalities such as a long face, large forehead and ears, and flat feet.

FXS occurs when the FMR1 gene, located at the end of the X chromosome, is turned off.

Healthy people have up to 55 repeats of “CGG” at the promoter of their FMR1 gene, all of which are unmethylated—these nucleotides don't have methyl groups attached.

Carriers of the FXS premutation have about 55 to 200 unmethylated CGGs.

People with the full mutation have over 200 CGG repeats, leading to methylation of the promoter region, which silences the gene.

Recently, researchers hypothesized that they could use CRISPR/Cas9, a gene editing technology, to demethylate that region, reactivate FMR1, and cure fragile X syndrome in mice.
