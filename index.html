What is fragile X syndrome?

Throughout the world, 1 in 3600 males and 1 in 4000 females suffer from fragile X syndrome (FXS), an inherited developmental disorder.

Symptoms range from intellectual disability and aggressive, hyperactive behavior to seizues and physical abnormalities such as a long face, large forehead and ears, and flat feet.

A boy with fragile X syndrome. Though individuals express different symptoms, frequent characteristics include developmental delays, a prominent jaw, and elongated face.

By Peter Saxon [CC BY-SA 4.0], from Wikimedia Commons

FXS occurs when the FMR1 gene, located at the end of the X chromosome, is turned off.

Healthy people have up to 55 repeats of “CGG” at the promoter of their FMR1 gene, all of which are unmethylated—these nucleotides don't have methyl groups attached.

Carriers of the FXS premutation have about 55 to 200 unmethylated CGGs.

People with the full mutation have over 200 methylated CGG repeats, which silences the gene (National Fragile X Foundation, 2017).

What's the new idea?

Previous evidence has suggested that it's not the presence of excess CGG repeats alone that cause FXS, but rather the methylation and subsequent silencing of FMR1 (Berman et al., 2014).

With that in mind, researchers at the Whitehead Institute in Cambridge, MA, and their collaborators hypothesized that they could use CRISPR/Cas9, a gene editing technology, to demethylate the CGG repeats, reactivate FMR1, and cure fragile X syndrome in mice models.

How does the cure work?

CRISPR/Cas9 is a recently developed gene editing tool. It's composed of two parts: a Cas9 enzyme and a guide RNA molecule. Cas9 acts as a pair of “scissors” that cuts DNA at specific positions, and the guide RNA, as you might guess, navigates the system to the part of the genome you care about (Hsu, Lander, & Zhang, 2014).

By Marius Walter [CC BY-SA 4.0], from Wikimedia Commons

First, the researchers created a special CRISPR/Cas9 system by attaching DNA methyltransferase (Dnmt) and ten-eleven translocation (Tet) enzymes, both of which can modify methylation, to Cas9. They then inserted it into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), which are somatic, or non-reproductive, cells that have been reverted back to unspecialized states (Liu et al., 2018).

In this case, the iPSCs were derived from mouse brain cells, and they were delivered to the cells through lentiviruses (Liu et al., 2018), which can integrate its genetic code into the genome of host species. In most cases, that's a dangerous characteristic, but in gene therapy, it makes these viruses useful as vectors, or vehicles for genetic material.

What are the results?

As a control, the experimenters used an inactivated Tet1 enzyme with the same guide RNA. The cells containing active Tet enzymes had expression levels of FMR1 restored to 90.4% of that of wild-type, or healthy, human embryonic stem cells. The control, however, showed no restoration.

Levels of methylation of the CGG repeats were reduced from 100% to just 4%. Another important result is that the off-target, or unexpected and unwanted, effects were minimal. Furthermore, restoration of FMR1 was also successfully performed and maintained directly in differentiated neuronal cells, in addition to iPSCs!

This is a major advancement from prior studies, which have largely only shown partial restoration of healthy phenotypes. By strategically pinpointing the exact mechanism that causes FXS and leveraging state of the art technology, scientists have demonstrated a promising cure.

What does this mean for the future?

In conclusion, CRISPR/Cas9-based technology proved to be an exceptionally successful treatment for fragile X syndrome in this comprehensive experiment, supporting the initial hypothesis.

With further studies, targeted demethylation of the FMR1 gene may eventually become a viable and valuable therapeutic option. For the millions of FXS carriers, patients, and their families throughout the world, this has the potential to relieve enormous psychological and financial burdens (Abbeduto et al., 2004).
